The Tinted Lens

Childlike Gaze and Unending Wonders

June 11, 2026 • Reflection

It has been a month since I returned from Bhutan. For as long as I can remember, this country has lived in my head as this bizarre idea—that I would be extremely happy and at peace. Over many years, Bhutan had become my haven of dreams, a sanctuary my mind returned to whenever the present felt unbearably complicated. But visiting Bhutan was entirely different from imagining it. The real Bhutan is far more nuanced, layered, and unexpected.

This April, I spent 9 days there, without family or friends, just with two other people—one an acquaintance and one a complete stranger. I must say, it was quite an adventure. We hiked through forests where mist clung to trees like a living thing, explored ancient monasteries perched impossibly on cliff edges, and navigated the winding roads of the mountains with both exhilaration and terror.

Never in my life have I seen such a thinly populated place, with empty land as far as your sight goes. Beautiful valleys, mountains embedded with small waterfalls, huge rivers, winding paths, and prayer flags scattered across the landscape like fragments of wishes. Bhutan felt pristine, untouched, and deeply spiritual. Yet something unexpected happened as I traveled through these breathtaking landscapes.

But somewhere in my travels through those winding mountain passes, I stumbled upon a question that has stayed with me. After visiting numerous temples and dzongs, I often heard people speak about the "power" of these places—how they exude spirituality, how they transform people, how they are unlike anywhere else in the world. And while these places are undoubtedly stunning, I began to wonder: Is the magic truly in the place itself, or is it in the act of experiencing something new?

But this made me question something broader: Aren't all landscapes, beaches, riverbanks, and mountains similar if you view them this way? Is the beauty of a place truly objective? Or is it simply the freshness of novelty—the fact that we've never seen it before, that it surprises us, that we're experiencing it for the first time?

"Perhaps this is where our perspective matters most—the ability to find beauty and wonder in everything, no matter how familiar or similar it might seem."

While there are undeniably some exceptionally beautiful places with unique experiences, what about the others? Isn't each experience inherently different and meaningful in its own right? A simple sunset by a local lake is fundamentally similar to a sunset in the mountains of Bhutan—both involve the sky changing colors, the light fading, the day concluding. Yet one feels magical because we've traveled thousands of miles to witness it, while the other feels ordinary because it's close to home.

I wish I had the ability to approach the world with unending wonder—to see everything with the awe of a child's gaze, as if experiencing it for the very first time. I never want to lose that ability to find magic in the mundane, beauty in the ordinary, and significance in the simple moments that make up a life.

The childlike gaze doesn't ask if you've seen it before. It only sees what's in front of you, and it is always enough.

Every place, every moment, seen anew.
