The Tinted Lens

Childlike Gaze and Unending Wonders

June 11, 2026 • Reflection

It has been a month since I returned from Bhutan. For as long as I can remember, this country has lived in my head as this bizarre idea—that I would be extremely happy and at peace there, despite knowing nothing much about the place, its culture, people, or food.

This April, I spent 9 days there, without family or friends, just with two other people—one an acquaintance and one a complete stranger. I must say, it was quite an adventure.

Never in my life have I seen such a thinly populated place, with empty land as far as your sight goes. Beautiful valleys, mountains embedded with small waterfalls, huge rivers, twisting roads, shrines, dzongs, really sweet people, and such serenity all around. Definitely one of the most beautiful places I have been to.

But somewhere in my travels through those winding mountain passes, I stumbled upon a question that has stayed with me. After visiting numerous temples and dzongs, I often heard people remark that they all look the same—that if you've seen one, there's no need to visit another. And I must admit, from the outside, many of these places do appear similar. If you're not deeply interested in their history, they might indeed feel repetitive.

But this made me question something broader: Aren't all landscapes, beaches, riverbanks, and mountains similar if you view them this way? Is the beauty of a place truly objective?

"Perhaps this is where our perspective matters most—the ability to find beauty and wonder in everything, no matter how familiar or similar it might seem."

While there are undeniably some exceptionally beautiful places with unique experiences, what about the others? Isn't each experience inherently different and meaningful in its own right? If you've been to one place, does that stop you from appreciating other, similar places?

I wish I had the ability to approach the world with unending wonder—to see everything with the awe of a child's gaze, as if experiencing it for the very first time. I never want to grow weary of the beauty of places. No matter how many beaches I visit or mountains I climb, no matter how many temples I encounter, I want to cherish them all with the same sense of amazement and appreciation.

The childlike gaze doesn't ask if you've seen it before. It only sees what's in front of you, and it is always enough.

Every place, every moment, seen anew.
