The Tinted Lens

Childlike Gaze and Unending Wonders

June 11, 2026 • Reflection

It has been a month since I returned from Bhutan. For as long as I can remember, this country has lived in my head as this bizarre idea—that I would be extremely happy and at peace. I don't know where this came from exactly, but Bhutan felt like some sort of promised land to me.

This April, I spent 9 days there, without family or friends, just with two other people—one an acquaintance and one a complete stranger. I must say, it was quite an adventure.

Never in my life have I seen such a thinly populated place, with empty land as far as your sight goes. Beautiful valleys, mountains embedded with small waterfalls, huge rivers, twisting roads that make you hold your breath—and yet, barely a soul around. The emptiness is what struck me most.

But somewhere in my travels through those winding mountain passes, I stumbled upon a question that has stayed with me. After visiting numerous temples and dzongs, I often heard people speak about sacred spaces—places that hold a special energy, a profound beauty that's incomparable. And I wondered: what makes these places sacred? Is it truly the place itself, or is it something we bring to it?

But this made me question something broader: Aren't all landscapes, beaches, riverbanks, and mountains similar if you view them this way? Is the beauty of a place truly objective? Or is it the lens through which we see it?

"Perhaps this is where our perspective matters most—the ability to find beauty and wonder in everything, no matter how familiar or similar it might seem."

While there are undeniably some exceptionally beautiful places with unique experiences, what about the others? Isn't each experience inherently different and meaningful in its own right, simply because we are experiencing it?

I wish I had the ability to approach the world with unending wonder—to see everything with the awe of a child's gaze, as if experiencing it for the very first time. I never want to lose that.

The childlike gaze doesn't ask if you've seen it before. It only sees what's in front of you, and it is always enough.

Every place, every moment, seen anew.
